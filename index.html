Read first — this is built from research, not a clinical exam

A herniated disc is individual. Get this cleared by your physio or doctor before running it. Two things matter most, and only a professional can assess them:

  • Your directional preference. Most posterior herniations feel better with extension and worse with flexion — but ~15–20% are the opposite. This decides whether the rehab work is medicine or aggravation. Don't guess.
  • Symptom rule, non-negotiable: pain that travels down the leg (numbness, tingling, sciatica = peripheralization) is a STOP signal — cut that movement. Pain that pulls back toward the midline (centralization) is the good direction.

The whole design principle: load itself isn't the enemy — load through a rounded, flexed spine is. So the spine stays out of the loaded chain entirely (seated, chest-supported, machine movements) while your legs and back still work hard.

Seven principles doing the work

Every exercise choice, rep range and effort cue below traces back to these. Understand them once and the program explains itself.

Structure

The week

Four sessions, each muscle trained twice — a heavier compound one day, a lengthened / isolation emphasis the other. Off-days carry daily rehab.

The training

Your four sessions

Effort is load-dependent — leave reps in reserve on the heavy compounds, take machines and isolation to failure. Rest 2–3 min on hard sets.

The part that heals

Daily rehab & supportive work

Done on off-days and as a warm-up. Costs almost nothing and builds a spine that protects every lift.

Daily + warm-up — descending pyramid 6/4/2

🧱 McGill Big 3

Open every session with these. Each rep is a hold under ~10 seconds, ~20–30s rest, descending reps per side (e.g. 6/4/2). Brace 360° (push abs out against your fingers), never hollow. Progress by adding reps, never longer holds. Not first thing on waking — wait ~1 hr out of bed.

  • Curl-up — hands under your lumbar arch, lift head/shoulders a couple inches. No crunching.
  • Side plank — knees bent (easier) or from the feet; straight line shoulder–hip–ankle.
  • Bird dog — neutral spine, extend opposite arm + leg, no arching or twisting.
Daily movement

🚶 Walking & decompression

  • Walk 20–40 min daily, progressive — first-line for disc health (WalkBack RCT, Lancet 2024). Treat it as real volume, not a warm-up afterthought.
  • Dead hangs (optional) — easy, 2–3 × 20–30s, only if it feels good.
  • McKenzie press-ups — only if your PT confirms you're extension-biased. 1–2 × 10, a few times a day. If extension worsens leg symptoms, skip entirely.
  • Warm-up: 5 min walk → Big 3 → hip/glute activation → ramp-up sets. No static lumbar stretching on an acute disc.
The dose

Weekly set tally

Anchor to ~10–16 direct sets/muscle/week — start near 10–12 and progress into the high-teens only if recovery and symptoms allow (no proven hard ceiling at 20). Count overlapping work fractionally so high frequency doesn't quietly overshoot. If a muscle stalls, add one set — don't start bloated.

Getting stronger

Double progression

The whole engine of getting bigger over time. Earn the weight before you add it.

Pick a load you can hit the bottom of the rep range for at the target RIR.

Each session, add reps at that load until you hit the top of the range on all sets.

Then add load (smallest jump available) and reset to the bottom of the range.

Earn it — beat last session's reps before adding weight. Add a set to a muscle only when its progress stalls and you're recovering well.

Symptom-gate every increase. Green-light more load only after a fully pain-free session with clean technique. Heavier on fresh days, lighter (still near failure) when fatigued. Any move that sends pain down the leg gets cut. Think in 12–16 week blocks.

📉 Deload

Every 5–6 weeks (sooner if performance drops two sessions running or the back is grumpy): halve the volume (≈1 set per exercise), keep the same days and loads. One week, then resume. Frequency stays — volume is what you pull back. Or use reactive deloading: just drop a single stalling lift rather than the whole week.

Evidence & references

This v2 was pressure-tested by a 47-agent research sprint that read primary sources, cross-checked every claim, and flagged the contested ones. Here's what changed and what backs it.

What v2 upgraded

⬆️ Research-backed changes

  • Effort capped at 1–3 RIR on most sets; true failure only on spine-neutral machines/isolation. Failure isn't superior for growth (Refalo 2022, ES 0.12, ns).
  • Frequency distributes volume, doesn't add it — it's neutral once weekly sets are equated (Schoenfeld 2019; Pelland 2025).
  • Weekly volume anchored to ~10–16 sets/muscle, fractional counting, progressed by symptoms — not a hard 20-set ceiling.
  • Lengthened partials as a last-set finisher only — equal to full ROM, not superior (Gschneidner 2025; Wolf 2025).
  • Symptom-gated load — centralizing pain OK, leg pain = cut the movement.
  • Walking promoted to its own pillar (WalkBack RCT, Lancet 2024).
Honest science

⚖️ What's contested

  • The "only the last ~5 reps count" effective-reps model (Beardsley/Carter) is a useful heuristic for why effort matters — but it's disputed (Stronger By Science). Not settled fact.
  • No proven hard volume ceiling at 20 sets — growth keeps rising with diminishing returns (Pelland 2025). The 10–16 band is a starting point to progress within.
  • "Flexion is THE cause of herniation" is from an in-vitro porcine model — the cautious reading (avoid loaded lumbar flexion) holds, but it's not iron law (Callaghan & McGill 2001).
Sources (real, verified)

A note on the science. The "low volume is enough / only the last few reps count" model is Beardsley's and Carter's genuine position and is the basis of this program — but it's contested; the higher-volume camp shows gains still rising into 10–20 sets/muscle/week. This program sits in the overlap where both camps agree you grow, at the lowest time and — critically here — the lowest spinal cost. The spine-safe choices draw on Stuart McGill's work, Barbell Medicine, and PT/biomechanics sources; the genuinely debated exercises were left out on purpose. None of this replaces an exam by your physio.
